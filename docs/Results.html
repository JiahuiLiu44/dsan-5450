Result

This section presents the results of the comparative textual analysis of the privacy policies of Flo, Clue, and Ovia. The results are organized in two stages. First, each app is analyzed separately using the same coding categories developed in the methods section. Second, the three cases are compared to identify broader patterns in how femtech apps communicate data collection, third-party sharing, advertising, analytics, retention, and sensitive reproductive health data practices.

The purpose of this section is not only to summarize what each privacy policy says, but also to evaluate whether the disclosures are specific and understandable enough to support meaningful informed consent. All three apps provide formal privacy disclosures, but the clarity, scope, and usability of those disclosures vary significantly.

Flo,Clue,Ovia

Flo

Flo’s privacy policy is highly detailed and provides substantial information about the types of personal and reproductive health data it collects. It explicitly discusses menstrual cycle details, pregnancy-related information, symptoms, sex life, physical and mental health, and data imported from third-party health platforms such as Apple HealthKit and Google Health Connect. Flo also provides detailed explanations of advertising and analytics partners, including AppsFlyer, Firebase, and TikTok Ad Manager.

However, Flo’s policy is also complex. Its explanations of third-party data processing, advertising, analytics, legal bases, and consent settings require users to understand multiple data flows across several external platforms. This creates a tension between formal disclosure and practical transparency: Flo discloses a lot, but the amount and complexity of the disclosure may still make it difficult for ordinary users to fully understand how their data is used.

Coding Category Flo
What sensitive data is collected? Flo collects general account information such as name, email, birth month and year, password, location, time zone, and language. More importantly, it collects reproductive and well-being data, including weight, height, BMI, body temperature, menstrual cycle details, pregnancy-related information, symptoms linked to menstrual cycle, pregnancy, perimenopause, or menopause, sex life, physical health, mental health, water intake, and sleep duration.
How is data collection explained? Flo explains that personal data can come directly from the user, automatically from device and app usage, from third-party services such as Apple HealthKit and Google Health Connect, and from external sources. This structure helps separate user-provided data from automatically collected data and imported data.
Is sensitive reproductive data clearly labeled? Partly yes. Flo explicitly refers to “special category data” and names menstrual cycle details, pregnancy-related information, symptoms, sex life, and physical and mental health. This makes the sensitive nature of the data relatively visible. However, these categories are still embedded within a long policy that may be difficult for users to process.
Is third-party sharing mentioned? Yes. Flo states that it does not share personal data with third parties except as described in the policy. It discusses sharing with service providers, advertising and analytics platforms, commercial partners, telehealth partners, research institutions, and other processors.
Is advertising mentioned? Yes. Flo gives detailed explanations of advertising and promotional data processing. It discusses AppsFlyer, Firebase, TikTok Ad Manager, Google services, ad networks, and Apple AppTrackingTransparency. Flo states that it does not share health data with third parties for marketing or advertising purposes, but it may share technical identifiers, subscription status, age group, app launch activity, and advertising identifiers for promotional purposes.
Is analytics mentioned? Yes. Flo discusses analytics in relation to app performance, promotional campaigns, advertising optimization, usage trends, Firebase, AppsFlyer, TikTok Ad Manager, and Google services. It also says usage activity may be reviewed to make sure the app works correctly, fix issues, serve relevant content, and improve user experience.
What vague phrases appear? Flo uses several broad or flexible phrases, including “may collect,” “may share,” “legitimate interests,” “improve our Services,” “enhance your experience,” “partners,” “business acquisition, transfer or reorganisation,” “as required by applicable law,” “in some cases,” and “other similar unique technical identifiers.”
Does it have a plain-language summary? Partly. Flo uses headings, examples, tables, and visual diagrams to explain some third-party data flows. This is helpful, especially for advertising and analytics. However, the policy remains long and complex, and users must move across multiple sections to understand the full picture.
Data retention Flo states that it keeps personal data for as long as necessary to provide services or fulfill the purposes for which it was collected. If a user deactivates the account or requests deletion, Flo says it processes the request within one month, although full deletion from backup systems may take up to 90 days. If the app is deleted or the account becomes inactive, Flo retains personal data for three years before deletion unless the user requests earlier deletion.
Security measures Flo states that it uses encryption in transit and at rest, vulnerability scanning, data integrity protection, organizational and legal measures, and privacy assessments. However, it also states that it cannot guarantee absolute security.

Preliminary Finding: Flo

Flo provides one of the most detailed privacy disclosures among the three apps. It is relatively explicit about the types of sensitive reproductive health data it collects, and it gives detailed descriptions of advertising and analytics partners. This level of detail is important because it gives users more information than a generic privacy policy would.

At the same time, detail does not automatically equal clarity. Flo’s policy requires users to understand distinctions between health data, device data, advertising identifiers, service providers, processors, commercial partners, and telehealth partners. Even when Flo states that it does not share health data with advertising partners, users must still interpret how other data types, such as age group, subscription status, app launch activity, IP address, and advertising identifiers, may move through advertising and analytics systems. Therefore, Flo demonstrates the central problem of this project: formal disclosure exists, but meaningful informed consent may still be limited by complexity.

Clue

Clue’s privacy policy is the most clearly organized of the three policies analyzed. It separates the data it processes into three main categories: account data, usage data, and health data. Clue defines health data as sensitive information that users choose to track in the app, including cycle information, period length, pain, spotting, weight, body temperature, hair quality, and sexual intercourse. It also explains that health data may be entered manually or imported from integrations such as Apple Health or supported fitness trackers. :contentReferenceoaicite:0

Compared with Flo and Ovia, Clue’s privacy policy is more explicit about separating health data from other types of data. It also repeatedly states that sensitive health data is not shared with advertisers. However, the policy is still complex because Clue relies on many third-party providers for hosting, communication, monitoring, customer support, advertising optimization, website analytics, surveys, and research.

Coding Category Clue
What sensitive data is collected? Clue collects health data and other sensitive data that users choose to track in the app. This includes cycle information such as period length, pain, spotting, and depending on what the user provides, weight, body temperature, hair quality, and sexual intercourse. It also collects account data such as username, date of birth, and email address. :contentReferenceoaicite:1
How is data collection explained? Clue explains data collection through three main categories: account data, usage data, and health data. Usage data includes device data, browser data, IP address, approximate location, crash information, and app interaction data. Health data can be entered manually or imported from integrations such as Apple Health or supported fitness trackers. :contentReferenceoaicite:2
Is sensitive reproductive data clearly labeled? Yes. Clue directly labels reproductive and cycle-tracking information as “Health data.” This category explicitly includes cycle information and other sensitive tracked experiences. This makes the reproductive health data category relatively clear compared with policies that rely mostly on broad terms such as “personal information” or “sensitive information.”
Is third-party sharing mentioned? Yes. Clue names multiple third-party providers, including AWS, Braze, Datadog, Firebase Cloud Messaging, SparkPost, Slack, Zendesk, Growthdot, research partners, Apple Search Ads, Google Ads, Adjust, Google Analytics, and Snowplow. Some providers, such as Braze, Datadog, Firebase Cloud Messaging, and SparkPost, are described as essential and cannot be opted out of. :contentReferenceoaicite:3
Is advertising mentioned? Yes. Clue says it processes usage data and technical data, such as device ID and IP address, to optimize advertising. It uses Apple Search Ads, Google Ads, and Adjust. Through Adjust, Clue shares standard app events such as app installation or Clue Plus subscription with ad networks such as Meta and TikTok. However, Clue states that Adjust does not have access to tracked personal health data. :contentReferenceoaicite:4
Is analytics mentioned? Yes. Clue discusses health analytics, technical app improvement, website analytics, advertising analytics, and research analytics. For health analytics, Clue states that processing is managed internally and that no third-party providers are used for that purpose. For website analytics, Clue uses cookies and third-party tracking services such as Google Analytics. :contentReferenceoaicite:5 :contentReferenceoaicite:6
What vague phrases appear? Clue uses some broad phrases, including “carefully selected research partners,” “improve our services,” “personalized recommendations,” “relevant products,” “certain third-party providers,” “legitimate interest,” “as soon as it is no longer required,” and “material to your consent.” These phrases are less vague than some terms in the other policies, but they still require interpretation.
Does it have a plain-language summary? Partly yes. Clue uses a relatively readable structure and separates data types clearly. It also provides short consent statements that users may toggle on or off in privacy settings. However, the full policy is still long and includes many third-party providers, legal bases, and data processing purposes.
Data retention Clue repeatedly states that personal data collected for each purpose is deleted as soon as it is no longer required for the purpose for which it was collected. It also states that users can request complete deletion of their data, including past data sent to third-party services used for tracking and analysis, and that deletion will occur within one month. :contentReferenceoaicite:7
Security measures Clue states that account/profile data is stored separately from health data, giving health data a higher level of protection. It also stores data on EU servers, uses password hashing and salting, HTTPS encrypted transmission, and does not store payment information. :contentReferenceoaicite:8

Preliminary Finding: Clue

Clue’s policy is comparatively strong in terms of structure and clarity. By separating account data, usage data, and health data, Clue makes it easier for users to understand that reproductive health information is different from ordinary account or device information. Clue also repeatedly emphasizes that tracked health data is not shared with advertisers, which directly addresses one of the major ethical concerns surrounding femtech apps.

However, Clue’s policy still illustrates the limits of privacy disclosure as a consent mechanism. Even though the structure is clearer than Flo’s and Ovia’s, the policy still describes many third-party providers and different processing purposes. Some tools are essential and cannot be opted out of. Other forms of processing depend on toggles or consent settings. As a result, users still need to understand a fairly complex data ecosystem in order to make an informed choice.

Ovia

Ovia’s privacy policy has the broadest scope of the three policies. This is because Ovia is not only a fertility and pregnancy app, but also provides parenting features, health coaching, employer or health-plan sponsored Ovia+ services, research-related uses, and deidentified or aggregated data services. Ovia’s policy explicitly lists fertility, pregnancy, health, sex life, life circumstances, child health and milestone information, legally protected classifications, biometric information, geolocation, inferences, and sensitive information. :contentReferenceoaicite:9

Ovia’s policy is especially important for this project because it shows how privacy disclosure becomes more complicated when reproductive health apps are connected to employers, health plans, health care providers, research partners, and commercial data uses. The policy states that Ovia no longer shows advertising to consumer users and does not sell or share personal data for advertising as of June 2025, but it also includes a historical table describing categories of data that were previously sold or shared for advertising. :contentReferenceoaicite:10

Coding Category Ovia
What sensitive data is collected? Ovia collects fertility, pregnancy, health, sex life, and life circumstances information that users input into the apps or discuss through health coaching. In the Parenting app, it may collect health and milestone information about a child. It also lists sensitive information such as racial or ethnic origin, health information, sex life, and sexual orientation information. :contentReferenceoaicite:11
How is data collection explained? Ovia organizes collected data into categories such as identifiers, general personal information including health information, legally protected classifications, biometric information, internet and app activity, geolocation, audio and visual information, professional and employment-related information, inferences, and sensitive information. This categorical structure is useful, but the scope is very broad. :contentReferenceoaicite:12
Is sensitive reproductive data clearly labeled? Partly. Ovia explicitly names fertility, pregnancy, health, sex life, pregnancy status, due date, and date of last period. However, these data points are often placed inside broad categories such as “general personal information including health information” and “sensitive information,” rather than being separated into a narrower reproductive health data category.
Is third-party sharing mentioned? Yes. Ovia mentions employers, health plans, health care providers, AWS, U.S.-based vendors, research partners such as universities and medical research institutions, other businesses that engage Ovia for research services, advertisers and sponsors for past promotions, and advertising or analytics providers before Ovia ended its advertising business. :contentReferenceoaicite:13 :contentReferenceoaicite:14
Is advertising mentioned? Yes. Ovia states that as of June 2025 it no longer shows advertising to consumer users and does not sell or share personal data for advertising. However, the policy also includes a historical table showing that before ending its advertising business, Ovia sold or shared certain categories of personal data for advertising, including online identifiers, IP addresses, app/ad engagement information, and in some opt-in promotions, pregnancy due month/year. :contentReferenceoaicite:15 :contentReferenceoaicite:16
Is analytics mentioned? Yes. Ovia says it uses personal data to understand how users use the Services, including what content and ads they view; obtain analytics about app performance; market, develop, and improve Ovia’s and its affiliates’ products and services; create deidentified and aggregated data; and conduct clinical and scientific research. :contentReferenceoaicite:17
What vague phrases appear? Ovia uses broad phrases such as “Comprehensive Use,” “legitimate interests,” “lawful conduct of its business,” “uses not permitted by law,” “other businesses,” “research and development purposes,” “as permitted by law,” “proper business records,” and “where required by law.” The phrase “Comprehensive Use” is especially important because it applies many purposes across multiple data categories.
Does it have a plain-language summary? Partly. Ovia has a shorter “Notice of Collection” and uses tables to organize categories of data. However, the policy is still complicated because it distinguishes between Consumer and Ovia+ users, includes employer and health-plan relationships, discusses deidentified data and aggregated data, and includes a historical advertising-sharing table.

Preliminary Finding: Ovia

Ovia’s policy raises the most complex informed consent issues because its data ecosystem is broader than a standard menstrual tracking app. Ovia collects and processes data not only for app functionality, but also for fertility, pregnancy, parenting, health coaching, employer or health-plan benefits, research, deidentified data, aggregated data, and product development. This makes the privacy policy harder to interpret because users must understand whether they are using the Consumer version or Ovia+, whether their data is connected to an employer or health plan, and how deidentified or aggregated data may be shared or monetized.

Ovia’s statement that it no longer sells or shares personal data for advertising is important. However, the policy’s historical advertising table shows that advertising-related sharing existed before June 2025. This matters because it demonstrates how privacy practices can change over time and how users may need to understand not only current data practices but also past or transitional practices. Ovia’s policy therefore shows how privacy disclosure can become especially burdensome when reproductive health data is connected to multiple institutional and commercial contexts.

Cross-Case Comparison

The three privacy policies show that formal disclosure is present across all cases. Flo, Clue, and Ovia each provide a public privacy policy, identify categories of collected data, discuss third-party processing, mention advertising or analytics, and describe retention or deletion practices. However, the policies differ substantially in how clearly they communicate these practices.

App Main Pattern
Flo Provides detailed explanations of sensitive data collection and advertising/analytics partners, but the policy is long and complex.
Clue Provides the clearest structure by separating account data, usage data, and health data, and repeatedly states that tracked health data is not shared with advertisers.
Ovia Has the broadest data scope because it includes fertility, pregnancy, parenting, health coaching, Ovia+, employer/health-plan contexts, research, and deidentified or aggregated data.

Result

Result 1: All Three Apps Disclose Sensitive Reproductive Health Data Collection

All three apps disclose that they collect sensitive reproductive or health-related data. Flo explicitly lists menstrual cycle details, pregnancy-related information, symptoms, sex life, and physical and mental health information. Clue identifies health data as a separate category and includes cycle information, period length, pain, spotting, weight, body temperature, hair quality, and sexual intercourse. Ovia includes fertility, pregnancy, health, sex life, life circumstances, child health and milestone information, and legally protected classifications.

The main difference is not whether the apps disclose sensitive data collection, but how they organize and explain it. Clue provides the clearest categorization by separating account data, usage data, and health data. Flo provides detailed examples, but the information is spread across a long and complex policy. Ovia lists many categories, but its broad structure makes it harder to isolate reproductive health data from other sensitive or institutional data uses.

Result 2: Third-Party Sharing Is Disclosed, but Data Flows Are Complex

Each policy mentions third-party providers, but the complexity of third-party relationships differs. Flo discusses advertising and analytics partners in detail, including AppsFlyer, Firebase, TikTok Ad Manager, Google services, commercial partners, telehealth partners, and processors. Clue identifies a wide range of third-party providers, including AWS, Braze, Datadog, Firebase Cloud Messaging, SparkPost, Slack, Zendesk, Adjust, Google Analytics, and research partners. Ovia discusses AWS, vendors, employers, health plans, health care providers, research partners, other businesses, and historical advertising partners.

These disclosures are important because they show that femtech data practices are not limited to the app company alone. Data may move through hosting providers, analytics tools, communication platforms, advertising attribution systems, research partners, and institutional partners. Even when companies state that health data is not shared with advertisers, users must still understand how other data types, such as device identifiers, IP addresses, usage events, subscription status, or inferred information, may be shared.

Result 3: Advertising and Analytics Are Treated Differently Across the Three Apps

Advertising and analytics are among the most important areas for informed consent. Flo provides the most detailed explanation of advertising and analytics partners and states that health data is not shared for advertising. Clue also states that tracked health data is not shared with advertisers and separates health analytics from advertising analytics. Ovia states that it no longer shows advertising to Consumer users and no longer sells or shares personal data for advertising as of June 2025, but its policy includes a historical table explaining previous advertising-related data sharing.

These differences show that advertising disclosure is not simply a yes-or-no issue. A policy may say that health data is not shared with advertisers, while still allowing technical identifiers, IP addresses, app events, or advertising identifiers to be processed for campaign optimization. This distinction is important, but it may not be obvious to ordinary users. Users may not clearly understand the difference between “health data,” “usage data,” “technical data,” “device identifiers,” and “advertising identifiers.”

Result 4: Broad and Ambiguous Language Appears in All Three Policies

All three policies contain broad language that gives companies flexibility. Flo uses phrases such as “may share,” “legitimate interests,” “improve our Services,” and “partners.” Clue uses phrases such as “carefully selected research partners,” “relevant products,” “certain third-party providers,” and “as soon as it is no longer required.” Ovia uses especially broad terms such as “Comprehensive Use,” “legitimate interests,” “lawful conduct of its business,” “other businesses,” and “as permitted by law.”

These phrases do not necessarily mean that the companies are hiding information. In many cases, they may reflect legal requirements or the need to describe complex technical systems. However, from the perspective of meaningful informed consent, broad language creates a problem: it shifts interpretive burden onto users. Users must decide what these phrases mean, how broad the company’s discretion is, and whether their sensitive reproductive health data could be used in ways they did not expect.

Result 5: Formal Disclosure Does Not Guarantee Practical Transparency

The strongest overall finding is that the existence of a privacy policy does not guarantee meaningful informed consent. All three apps disclose important information, but the disclosures are long, technical, legalistic, and distributed across many sections. Clue is the clearest of the three, but even Clue requires users to understand multiple legal bases, third-party providers, consent toggles, and analytics categories. Flo provides detailed explanations but may overwhelm users with complexity. Ovia provides broad categorical disclosures, but its Consumer/Ovia+ distinction, institutional partnerships, deidentified data practices, and historical advertising table make the policy difficult to interpret.

This finding supports the central argument of the project: privacy policies should not be treated as equivalent to meaningful informed consent. In the context of reproductive health data, meaningful consent requires more than formal notice. It requires disclosures that are specific, readable, and usable for ordinary users.

Summary

Overall, the analysis shows that Flo, Clue, and Ovia all provide formal privacy disclosures, but they vary in clarity and usability. Clue offers the clearest data categorization and the strongest separation between health data and advertising data. Flo provides extensive detail, especially about advertising and analytics, but its policy may be difficult for ordinary users to navigate. Ovia has the broadest and most complex data environment because it includes pregnancy, parenting, health coaching, employer or health-plan services, research, deidentified data, aggregated data, and historical advertising practices.

  • This results applies the course concept of operationalization by turning the abstract idea of meaningful informed consent into observable textual indicators, including clarity around sensitive data collection, third-party sharing, advertising, analytics, retention, and vague policy language.
  • The result first makes descriptive claims about what Flo, Clue, and Ovia disclose in their privacy policies. It then makes normative recommendations about how femtech privacy disclosures should be redesigned to better support meaningful informed consent.
  • From a deontological perspective, meaningful consent is necessary to respect user autonomy. From a consequentialist perspective, unclear privacy disclosures can lead users to underestimate the risks of sharing sensitive reproductive health data.

The results suggest that the central privacy issue in femtech apps is not simply whether companies disclose data practices. The deeper issue is whether users can realistically understand those disclosures. This distinction is especially important because menstrual, fertility, pregnancy, sexual activity, and reproductive health data are highly sensitive. When privacy policies rely on long explanations, broad categories, and complex third-party relationships, user consent may be formally obtained but not meaningfully informed.
