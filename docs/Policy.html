Policy Recommendations

The findings of this project show that Flo, Clue, and Ovia all provide formal privacy disclosures, but formal disclosure alone is not enough to guarantee meaningful informed consent. Each app explains some aspects of data collection, third-party sharing, advertising, analytics, retention, and user choices. However, the policies are still long, complex, and difficult for ordinary users to fully understand. This is especially concerning because femtech apps collect highly sensitive reproductive health data, including menstrual cycle information, fertility data, pregnancy information, symptoms, sexual activity, physical and mental health information, and in some cases child-related or employer-related data.

The policy recommendations below are based on the central finding of this project: privacy policies should not be treated as equivalent to meaningful informed consent. Meaningful consent requires disclosures that are clear, specific, readable, and usable. In the context of femtech apps, regulators and companies should move beyond long legal privacy policies and create stronger standards for reproductive health data transparency.

Require Standardized Plain-Language Privacy Summaries

The first recommendation is that regulators should require femtech apps to provide a standardized plain-language privacy summary at the beginning of the privacy policy and inside the app before users agree to data processing.

The analysis shows that all three apps disclose important information, but the information is often spread across many sections. Flo provides detailed explanations of advertising and analytics partners, but the policy is complex. Clue is better organized, especially because it separates account data, usage data, and health data, but it is still long and contains many third-party providers and legal bases. Ovia provides a table and a shorter notice, but its policy is complicated because it covers Consumer users, Ovia+ users, employers, health plans, research partners, deidentified data, and aggregated data.

A plain-language summary would reduce the burden on users. Instead of requiring users to search through long legal documents, the app should provide a short standardized disclosure that answers the most important questions immediately.

A required femtech privacy summary should include:

Required Summary Item Explanation
What sensitive data is collected? The app should clearly list menstrual data, fertility data, pregnancy data, symptoms, sexual activity, health conditions, mental health information, and any child-related data.
Why is the data collected? The app should explain whether the data is used for core app functions, predictions, personalization, research, advertising, analytics, employer benefits, or product development.
Is the data shared with third parties? The app should identify categories of third parties, such as analytics providers, advertising platforms, cloud providers, research partners, employers, health plans, or health care providers.
Is the data used for advertising? The app should clearly state whether reproductive health data, usage data, technical identifiers, or inferences are used for advertising, ad attribution, or campaign optimization.
Is the data used for analytics or research? The app should explain whether data is used for health analytics, app analytics, scientific research, product development, deidentified datasets, or aggregated statistics.
How long is the data retained? The app should state a specific retention period or explain when data is deleted.
Can users delete or export their data? The app should explain how users can delete, correct, download, or opt out of certain processing.

The summary should be written at an accessible reading level. It should avoid legalistic phrases such as “as permitted by law,” “legitimate interests,” “business purposes,” or “Comprehensive Use” unless those terms are immediately explained in ordinary language.

This recommendation does not require companies to stop collecting data or offering personalized services. Instead, it requires them to communicate data practices in a way that ordinary users can understand. This directly addresses the problem identified in the literature review: users cannot meaningfully consent if the privacy information is too long, complex, or difficult to interpret.

Create Mandatory Sensitive Reproductive Data Labels

The second recommendation is that femtech apps should be required to label sensitive reproductive health data as a distinct category. Companies should not be allowed to hide menstrual, fertility, pregnancy, sexual activity, or reproductive health information inside broad categories such as “personal information,” “health information,” “general personal information,” or “sensitive information” without further explanation.

This recommendation is based on the comparison of Flo, Clue, and Ovia. Clue provides a relatively strong example because it separates data into account data, usage data, and health data. This makes it easier for users to see that reproductive health data is different from ordinary account or device data. Flo also explicitly identifies menstrual cycle details, pregnancy-related information, symptoms, sex life, and physical and mental health information as sensitive or special category data. Ovia names many sensitive data types, including fertility, pregnancy, health, sex life, sexual orientation, and pregnancy status, but these are often placed inside broad categories such as “general personal information including health information” and “sensitive information.”

A stronger policy standard would require femtech apps to use a clear label such as:

Sensitive Reproductive Health Data

Under this label, the app should list every relevant type of data collected. For example:

Sensitive Reproductive Health Data Type Collected? Example
Menstrual cycle data Yes/No Period dates, cycle length, spotting, pain
Fertility data Yes/No Ovulation estimates, fertile window, trying-to-conceive status
Pregnancy data Yes/No Due date, pregnancy week, pregnancy symptoms
Sexual activity data Yes/No Sexual intercourse, contraception, sex life information
Symptom data Yes/No Cramps, mood, pain, discharge, nausea
Health condition data Yes/No Medical conditions, mental health, physical health
Child or parenting data Yes/No Baby milestones, child health information
Inferred reproductive data Yes/No Pregnancy trimester, fertile window, predicted period

This label would help users recognize that reproductive health information deserves special attention. It would also prevent companies from making sensitive data less visible by placing it under broad legal categories. The goal is not only to disclose data collection, but to make the sensitivity of the data obvious.

This recommendation also connects to contextual integrity. Users may understand that a period tracking app needs period dates to provide predictions. However, they may not expect reproductive data or inferred reproductive status to be used for advertising, analytics, employer benefits, health plan services, research, or product development. A sensitive data label would help users understand the context in which their data is being collected and the contexts into which it may move.

Require a Standardized Femtech Data Use Table

The third recommendation is that regulators should require femtech apps to include a standardized data use table. This table should show, for each type of data, whether it is collected, why it is used, whether it is shared, whether it is used for advertising, whether it is used for analytics or research, and how long it is retained.

This recommendation comes directly from the findings of this project. One of the biggest problems across Flo, Clue, and Ovia is that users must move across many sections of the privacy policy to understand the full data flow. For example, a user may need to read one section about data collection, another about advertising, another about analytics, another about research, another about third-party providers, another about retention, and another about user rights. Even when the company discloses the information, the structure makes it difficult to compare and understand.

A standardized table would make privacy information more usable. The table could look like this:

Data Type Collected? Purpose Shared with Third Parties? Used for Ads? Used for Analytics or Research? Retention Period
Account data Yes Login, account management, communication Service providers No/Yes Yes/No Specific period
Menstrual cycle data Yes Cycle prediction, health insights Yes/No No/Yes Yes/No Specific period
Pregnancy data Yes Pregnancy tracking, personalization Yes/No No/Yes Yes/No Specific period
Sexual activity data Yes/No Health tracking, personalization Yes/No No/Yes Yes/No Specific period
Device identifiers Yes App function, analytics, advertising attribution Yes/No Yes/No Yes/No Specific period
IP address Yes Security, analytics, approximate location Yes/No Yes/No Yes/No Specific period
Inferences Yes/No Personalization, predictions, recommendations Yes/No Yes/No Yes/No Specific period
Deidentified data Yes/No Research, product development, statistics Yes/No No/Yes Yes/No Specific period

This type of table would make it easier for users to compare data practices across apps. It would also help researchers, journalists, and regulators evaluate whether companies are being clear about sensitive reproductive data.

The table should be mandatory and standardized across femtech apps. If every company uses a different format, users still face a comparison problem. Standardization would reduce confusion and make privacy disclosures more transparent.

Require Specific Disclosure of Third-Party Sharing

The fourth recommendation is that femtech apps should be required to disclose third-party sharing in a more specific way. It is not enough to say that data may be shared with “service providers,” “partners,” “vendors,” or “third parties.” These terms are too broad for users to understand the actual data flow.

The findings show that all three apps rely on third-party providers. Flo discusses advertising and analytics partners such as AppsFlyer, Firebase, TikTok Ad Manager, and Google services. Clue names providers such as AWS, Braze, Datadog, Firebase Cloud Messaging, SparkPost, Slack, Zendesk, Adjust, Google Analytics, and Snowplow. Ovia mentions AWS, U.S.-based vendors, employers, health plans, health care providers, research partners, and other businesses that may receive deidentified or aggregated data.

Because third-party ecosystems are complex, users need a clearer disclosure standard. Apps should be required to disclose:

  • the name or category of the third party;
  • the type of data shared;
  • the purpose of sharing;
  • whether sensitive reproductive health data is included;
  • whether the third party can use the data for its own purposes;
  • whether the data leaves the user’s country or legal jurisdiction;
  • whether users can opt out.

A standardized third-party sharing table could look like this:

Third Party Category Example Provider Data Shared Purpose Includes Reproductive Health Data? Opt-Out Available?
Cloud hosting provider AWS Account data, app data Storage and infrastructure Yes/No No
Analytics provider Google Analytics / Firebase / Adjust Usage data, device identifiers, IP address App or website analytics Yes/No Yes/No
Advertising partner TikTok, Meta, Google Ads Advertising identifiers, app events Campaign optimization Yes/No Yes/No
Customer support provider Zendesk / Slack Account data, support content User support Yes/No Yes/No
Research partner University or medical institution Deidentified health data Scientific research Yes/No Yes/No
Employer or health plan Employer/health plan partner Eligibility or benefit data Ovia+ services Yes/No Yes/No

This recommendation is important because third-party sharing is one of the hardest parts of privacy policies for users to understand. A user may not know the difference between a processor, controller, vendor, service provider, advertising network, or research partner. A specific table would make these relationships more visible.

This recommendation would also help reduce vague language. Instead of saying “we may share data with partners,” companies would need to explain what kind of partner, what kind of data, and for what purpose.

Separate Advertising, Analytics, and Core Service Processing

The fifth recommendation is that femtech apps should be required to clearly separate three different types of data processing:

  1. core service processing;
  2. analytics and product improvement;
  3. advertising and marketing.

These categories should not be blended together. Users should be able to understand which data is necessary for the app to function and which data is used for secondary purposes.

This recommendation is important because the analysis shows that advertising and analytics are disclosed differently across the three apps. Flo provides detailed explanations of advertising and analytics partners and states that health data is not shared for advertising. Clue states that tracked health data is not shared with advertisers and separates health analytics from advertising analytics. Ovia states that it no longer shows advertising to Consumer users and does not sell or share personal data for advertising as of June 2025, but its policy also includes a historical table explaining prior advertising-related sharing.

These distinctions are important, but they may be difficult for ordinary users to understand. Many users may not know the difference between health data, usage data, technical data, device identifiers, advertising identifiers, app events, and inferences. A company may say that it does not share “health data” with advertisers, but still use technical identifiers or app events for advertising attribution. This may be legally different, but from the user’s perspective it can still feel privacy-relevant.

Femtech apps should therefore provide a clear processing separation table:

Processing Type Required for App Function? Data Used Third Parties Involved User Choice
Core service Yes Account data, reproductive health data Hosting and essential service providers Required to use app
Health insights and predictions Usually yes Menstrual, fertility, pregnancy, symptom data Internal systems or limited providers Required/optional depending on feature
App analytics No/Partly Usage data, device data, crash data Analytics providers Opt-out should be available
Advertising attribution No Device identifiers, app events, ad IDs Advertising platforms and attribution providers Opt-in should be required
Research No Deidentified or consented health data Research partners Separate consent should be required
Employer or health-plan benefits No, except for Ovia+ Eligibility or benefit-related data Employer, health plan, provider partners Separate disclosure and consent required

This separation would make consent more meaningful because users could distinguish between data processing that is necessary and data processing that is optional or secondary. Users should not have to accept advertising, research, or broad analytics uses simply because they want basic period tracking or pregnancy support.

Require Separate Opt-In Consent for Secondary Uses of Reproductive Health Data

The sixth recommendation is that femtech apps should obtain separate opt-in consent for secondary uses of reproductive health data. Secondary uses include advertising, marketing, research, product development beyond core service delivery, deidentified data sharing, aggregated data sharing, employer or health-plan reporting, and commercial partnerships.

This recommendation responds directly to the ethical issue identified in the project: reproductive health data is context-sensitive. Users may provide menstrual or pregnancy data for personal health tracking, but that does not mean they expect the data to be used for advertising optimization, research partnerships, commercial datasets, or employer benefit services.

Separate opt-in consent should be required for:

  • sharing reproductive health data with research partners;
  • using reproductive health data to create deidentified datasets;
  • sharing or selling deidentified or aggregated data;
  • using reproductive health data for product development beyond core app functionality;
  • using related inferences for recommendations or marketing;
  • connecting app data to employer, health plan, or health care provider programs;
  • using technical or behavioral data for advertising attribution.

This opt-in should be specific. Instead of one broad consent statement, users should be able to make separate choices. For example:

Secondary Use Consent Required? Default Setting
Use health data for cycle predictions Yes, as part of core service On if user uses feature
Share deidentified health data for academic research Yes Off by default
Share deidentified or aggregated data with commercial partners Yes Off by default
Use device identifiers for advertising attribution Yes Off by default
Send personalized product recommendations based on health data Yes Off by default
Connect data to employer or health-plan benefits Yes Off by default

This recommendation would help protect user autonomy. It would also reduce the problem of bundled consent, where users must accept many types of data processing in order to use a basic service.

Strengthen Rules for Deidentified and Aggregated Data

The seventh recommendation is that femtech apps should provide clearer disclosure and stronger consent rules for deidentified and aggregated data. This is especially important because reproductive health data can remain sensitive even when identifiers are removed.

Ovia is the clearest example of why this matters. Its policy states that it may use personal data to create deidentified data for research and development, provide deidentified data to research partners, provide data to other businesses that engage Ovia for research services, disclose or sell deidentified data derived from patient information, and receive compensation for sharing deidentified or aggregate data. This is disclosed, but it raises important consent questions. Users may not understand that data they enter for fertility, pregnancy, or parenting support could later contribute to deidentified datasets used for research, product development, or compensated sharing.

Deidentification can reduce privacy risk, but it should not eliminate transparency obligations. Users should be told:

  • what data may be deidentified;
  • how deidentification is performed;
  • whether deidentified data may be shared;
  • who may receive it;
  • whether it may be sold or used for compensation;
  • whether it may be combined with other datasets;
  • whether users can opt out.

A stronger disclosure could look like this:

Question Required Disclosure
Will my reproductive health data be used to create deidentified data? Yes/No
What types of data may be included? Menstrual, fertility, pregnancy, symptoms, app usage, etc.
Who may receive deidentified data? Researchers, universities, medical institutions, commercial partners, internal teams
Can the company receive compensation? Yes/No
Can I opt out? Yes/No
Will the company attempt to reidentify me? No, with enforceable commitment

This recommendation is important because users may not understand the difference between personal data, deidentified data, and aggregated data. Companies should not rely on technical definitions alone. They should explain these concepts in plain language and provide meaningful choices.

Require Clearer Data Retention and Deletion Standards

The eighth recommendation is that femtech apps should provide clear and specific data retention periods. Users should not have to guess how long their reproductive health data will remain stored.

The three apps vary in how they describe retention. Flo says it retains data as long as necessary and may retain inactive account data for three years. Clue repeatedly says data is deleted when it is no longer required for the purpose for which it was collected, and users can request deletion within one month. Ovia provides a specific standard for Consumer users: it stores all categories of data while the account is active and for seven years afterward.

Specific retention periods are easier for users to understand than broad phrases such as “as long as necessary.” A stronger policy standard should require apps to provide a retention table:

Data Category Retention Period What Happens After Deletion Request? Backup Deletion Timeline
Account data Specific period Deleted or anonymized Specific timeline
Reproductive health data Specific period Deleted or anonymized Specific timeline
Device and usage data Specific period Deleted or anonymized Specific timeline
Advertising identifiers Specific period Deleted or anonymized Specific timeline
Research data Specific period or study duration Deleted, anonymized, or retained under consent Specific timeline
Deidentified data Specific period or indefinite Explain whether deletion applies Specific timeline

Data retention is central to informed consent because users may be comfortable sharing data temporarily but not indefinitely. For example, a user may use a pregnancy app during pregnancy but may not expect pregnancy-related data to remain stored for many years after the account becomes inactive. Femtech apps should therefore make retention periods visible and specific.

Users should also be able to delete their data inside the app without having to search for a support email. Data deletion should be available through an accessible account setting, and the app should confirm what data has been deleted, what data may remain, and why.

Require Special Protection for Employer, Health Plan, and Ovia+ Contexts

The ninth recommendation is that apps connected to employers, health plans, or health care providers should follow stricter disclosure standards. This recommendation is especially relevant to Ovia because its policy distinguishes between Consumer users and Ovia+ users, where Ovia+ may be provided through employers, health plans, or health care providers.

Employer and health plan contexts raise special privacy concerns. Users may worry that reproductive health information could affect employment, insurance, benefits, or institutional treatment, even if the company states that sensitive data is not shared in that way. Because of this power imbalance, ordinary privacy policy disclosure is not enough.

For apps connected to employers or health plans, users should receive a separate disclosure answering:

Question Required Answer
Is my employer involved? Yes/No
Is my health plan involved? Yes/No
What data does the employer or health plan receive? Specific categories
Does the employer or health plan receive individual-level health data? Yes/No
Does the employer or health plan receive aggregated reports? Yes/No
Can my app use affect my benefits? Yes/No
Can I use the app without employer or health-plan data sharing? Yes/No

This recommendation is based on the idea that consent is affected by context and power. A user using a consumer period tracker is in a different position from a user accessing an app through an employer-sponsored health benefit. In employer or health-plan settings, users may feel pressure to participate or may not fully understand what information flows back to the institution. Therefore, stronger disclosure and opt-in consent are necessary.

Require Auditable Privacy Claims

The tenth recommendation is that major privacy claims made by femtech apps should be auditable. If an app says “we do not share health data with advertisers,” “we do not sell personal data,” or “health data is deidentified before research sharing,” there should be a way for regulators or independent auditors to verify those claims.

This recommendation is important because privacy policies are self-reported documents. They describe what companies say they do, but users usually cannot verify the technical reality. This limitation was also identified in the methods section of this project. Textual analysis can evaluate disclosure quality, but it cannot independently confirm actual data flows inside the app.

Auditable privacy claims would help close the gap between policy language and technical practice. Regulators could require periodic independent audits for femtech apps that collect sensitive reproductive health data. These audits could examine:

  • whether app data flows match privacy policy claims;
  • whether health data is separated from advertising systems;
  • whether third-party SDKs receive sensitive data;
  • whether opt-out settings work correctly;
  • whether deletion requests are honored;
  • whether deidentified data is protected from reidentification;
  • whether employer or health-plan partners receive only the data described in the policy.

Audit summaries should be made public in plain language. Users should not need technical expertise to understand whether a company’s privacy claims have been independently reviewed.

Policy Implementation: A Femtech Privacy Disclosure Standard

The recommendations above could be implemented through a standardized femtech privacy disclosure rule. This rule could be developed by privacy regulators, consumer protection agencies, health data regulators, or industry standard-setting bodies. The goal would not be to ban femtech apps or prevent useful health tracking services. Instead, the goal would be to create a higher disclosure standard for apps that collect sensitive reproductive health data.

A femtech privacy disclosure standard should include:

  1. a plain-language summary;
  2. a sensitive reproductive health data label;
  3. a standardized data use table;
  4. a third-party sharing table;
  5. separate categories for core service, analytics, advertising, research, and employer/health-plan processing;
  6. opt-in consent for secondary uses;
  7. specific retention and deletion timelines;
  8. special disclosure for employer or health-plan contexts;
  9. stronger rules for deidentified and aggregated data;
  10. independent audit requirements for major privacy claims.

This standard would directly respond to the main weakness identified in the analysis: privacy policies may provide formal disclosure while still failing to provide practical transparency. A standardized disclosure system would make it easier for users to understand and compare privacy practices across femtech apps.

Summary

The main policy goal should be to move from formal notice to meaningful informed consent. Flo, Clue, and Ovia all provide privacy disclosures, but the findings show that users still face a heavy interpretation burden. The problem is not simply that companies fail to disclose information. The problem is that disclosures are often too complex, broad, or scattered to support realistic user understanding.

The following table summarizes the recommendations:

Recommendation Problem Addressed Expected Benefit
Standardized plain-language privacy summary Policies are too long and complex Helps users quickly understand key data practices
Sensitive reproductive data labels Sensitive data is sometimes placed in broad categories Makes reproductive health data visible as a special category
Standardized data use table Users must search across many sections Makes collection, use, sharing, ads, analytics, and retention easier to compare
Specific third-party sharing disclosure Terms like “partners” and “providers” are vague Shows who receives data and why
Separation of core service, analytics, and advertising Users may not understand different processing purposes Clarifies what is necessary and what is optional
Separate opt-in for secondary uses Consent is often bundled Gives users more meaningful control
Stronger rules for deidentified and aggregated data Users may not understand commercial or research uses Improves transparency around data reuse
Clear retention and deletion standards Retention language can be vague Helps users know how long data remains stored
Special protection for employer and health-plan contexts Institutional relationships create power concerns Protects users in benefit-based app programs
Auditable privacy claims Policies are self-reported Makes privacy promises more trustworthy

Overall, these recommendations would make femtech privacy policies more specific, comparable, and user-centered. They would also better align privacy disclosure with the sensitivity of reproductive health data. Meaningful informed consent should not depend on whether a user can interpret a long legal document. It should depend on whether the company provides clear, structured, and actionable information about how sensitive data is collected, shared, used, retained, and protected.
